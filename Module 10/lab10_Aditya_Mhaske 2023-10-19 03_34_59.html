In [ ]:

The provided visualization, which includes a scatter plot of the ratio data along with a horizontal reference line at a ratio of 1, is a reasonable way to represent the data, but it may not be the best choice depending on the specific context and the goals of the visualization.

Pros:

Clarity: The plot is clear and easy to interpret. It effectively shows the relationship between data IDs and ratio values.

Reference Line: The horizontal reference line at ratio=1 serves as a useful reference point for comparison, making it easier to identify data points above or below this threshold.

Cons:

Limited Insights: While the plot clearly displays the data points and their relationship to the reference line, it doesn't provide much additional insight or context about the data. For more complex datasets, additional visualizations or statistical analysis may be needed to draw meaningful conclusions.

Context Dependence: The suitability of this visualization depends on the specific goals and audience. If the goal is to compare ratios to a reference value, it works well. However, if the goal is to uncover patterns or trends in the data, other visualization types like line charts or box plots might be more informative.

In summary, this visualization is good for a simple comparison of ratios to a reference value but may need to be complemented with other visuals or analyses for a more comprehensive understanding of the data.

Q: Can you explain the plot? Why are there gaps?

In [ ]:
# YOUR SOLUTION HERE

X-Axis (World Wide Gross): The x-axis represents the "World Wide Gross" values of movies, which are presented in a logarithmic scale. This means that the spacing between values increases exponentially as you move to the right on the x-axis. The bins are designed to cover a wide range of gross values effectively.

Y-Axis (Probability Density): The y-axis represents the probability density. Probability density is a measure of the likelihood of a continuous random variable (in this case, "World Wide Gross") falling within a particular range of values. The y-values are also presented in a logarithmic scale.

Gaps: The gaps in the plot are due to the binning process. In a log-binned histogram, the bin edges are spaced logarithmically. This means that smaller bins are used to represent the lower values and larger bins for the higher values. As a result, there can be gaps in the histogram when there are no data points falling within certain bin ranges. This is typical when dealing with data that has a wide range of values and can be more easily visualized in log space. The gaps are a natural consequence of the logarithmic binning and scaling.

The plot is a way to represent the distribution of "World Wide Gross" values with a focus on highlighting the behavior in different ranges of the data, especially when the data spans several orders of magnitude. The logarithmic scaling helps in visualizing both low and high values effectively.

CCDF¶

CCDF is a nice alternative to examine distributions with heavy tails. The idea is same as CDF, but the direction of aggregation is opposite.

Q: Draw a CCDF of worldwide gross data in log-log scale

In [ ]:
# Implement

# YOUR SOLUTION HERE
movies["Worldwide_Gross"] = movies["Worldwide_Gross"] + 1.0
worldgross_sorted = np.sort(movies["Worldwide_Gross"])
N = len(worldgross_sorted)

Y = np.linspace(1.0, 1 / N, num=N)

plt.loglog(worldgross_sorted, Y)

plt.xlabel("World wide gross")
plt.ylabel("CCDF")
plt.show()

We can also try in semilog scale (only one axis is in a log-scale), where the horizontal axis is linear.

A straight line in semilog scale means exponential decay (cf. a straight line in log-log scale means power-law decay). So it seems like the amount of money a movie makes across the world follows roughly an exponential distribution, while there are some outliers that make insane amount of money.
